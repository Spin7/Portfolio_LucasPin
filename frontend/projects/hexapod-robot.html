Autonomous Hexapod Robot

Year: 2025

Overview

Complete development of a 6-legged autonomous robot (hexapod) — from mechanical 3D design and embedded firmware to a full ROS2 software stack running simultaneously in Gazebo simulation and on the physical robot. The same high-level behaviors — autonomous GPS navigation, vision-based following, swarm coordination, and hand-gesture interaction — run identically on both platforms. The only difference is the actuation layer.

Mechanical Design

Custom 3D-Printed Chassis

The entire mechanical structure was designed from scratch in SolidWorks. The assembly covers every sub-component — from the central body plates to the smallest servo mounting bracket — all parametrized for future modifications.

Each leg has three rigid segments connected by JS40F servo joints: the Coxa (51 mm) rotates the leg horizontally, the Femur (65 mm) controls the forward-backward angle, and the Tibia (121 mm) manages contact with the ground. A rubber foot bumper reduces slipping and absorbs impact.

3D-Printed Parts

  • Body plates — Top and bottom chassis plates with electronics mounting points
  • Body risers — Spacers that set the chassis height off the ground
  • Femur & Tibia brackets — Mid- and lower-leg structural elements
  • Servo mounts & bearing centers — Precision-fit fixtures for each JS40F joint
  • Foot plate & bumper — Ground-contact surface with non-slip properties
  • Wire guides — Cable management channels running along each leg
  • Electronics holders — Custom mounts for Raspberry Pi, SBEC, and receiver
  • Sensor supports — Brackets for HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensors

Electronics Layout

The Arduino Mega sits centrally on the bottom plate, with 18 servo signal wires routed through the leg guides to each joint. The Raspberry Pi mounts above via a dedicated printed support, connected to the Arduino over USB serial. Power flows from a 12 V LiPo battery through an SBEC voltage regulator to the servo rail.

Gazebo Simulation
Simulation — Gazebo Harmonic

Before any behavior runs on real hardware, it is fully validated in Gazebo Harmonic. The simulated hexapod is a faithful URDF replica of the physical robot — same kinematic chain, same joint limits, same sensor placement. High-level ROS2 nodes are completely unmodified; only the low-level actuation bridge is swapped. This makes simulation a first-class citizen of the development workflow, not an afterthought.

Social Robot — Hand Gesture Control

The hexapod responds to hand gestures captured by the camera. Using MediaPipe landmark detection, gestures are recognized and translated into symbolic robot actions: ROCK → aggressive pose, VICTORY → celebration sequence, AL_PELO → custom expressive motion — an experimental social interaction layer running in Gazebo.

Physical Robot
Real Hardware — Raspberry Pi + Arduino

Once validated in simulation, every mode was deployed on the physical robot without modifying a single high-level ROS2 node. The Raspberry Pi 4 onboard joins the DDS network over Wi-Fi, and the Arduino Mega takes over servo actuation. Real sensors, real noise, real terrain — a true end-to-end autonomous system.
