Smart Mechatronic Trap

Design and Implementation of an Edge AI–Based IoT Mosquito Trap for Real-Time Detection and Classification

Matteo Martinez · Lucas Pin · Diego Stalder · Christian Schaerer
Facultad de Ingeniería / Facultad Politécnica — Universidad Nacional de Asunción

Status Field-validated prototype
Year 2026
Context IEEE conference paper · LME–FIUNA · supported by CONACYT Paraguay & Wolbloc–Wellcome Trust

Resumen

Problemática

Dengue surveillance in tropical regions depends on accurate, high-resolution counts of female Aedes aegypti, the sole blood-feeding and disease-transmitting sex. Biological control strategies such as Wolbachia-based releases require continuous, sex-disaggregated monitoring to assess effectiveness, yet traditional traps rely on manual inspection, and existing automated traps either lack artificial-intelligence capabilities or cannot physically separate specimens by sex — a gap that limits their epidemiological usefulness.

Propuesta

This work presents a low-cost, edge–AI IoT mosquito trap that simultaneously captures, classifies, and physically sorts specimens by sex. A mechatronic rotary mechanism routes each insect into a sex-segregated compartment, guided by a cascaded YOLOv8 + MobileNetV3 pipeline exported to ONNX and running on a Raspberry Pi 5, alongside a three-layer IoT architecture for real-time cloud monitoring. The system reaches mAP@50 = 0.977 for detection and 97.31% accuracy for sex classification, with a full inference cycle of 133.53 ms. Field and semi-field trials confirmed real-world robustness, at a total prototype cost of USD 389.50 — a 6.6× reduction relative to the commercial BG-Counter reference.

II. Related Work

Commercial systems. Devices such as the BG-Counter detect insect activity through optical and infrared sensors with remote data transmission, but rely on classical signal processing rather than deep learning, and are not capable of physically segregating specimens by sex.

Computer-vision approaches. Park et al. compared VGG-16, ResNet-50, and SqueezeNet on a Jetson TX2 platform, showing via Grad-CAM heatmaps that CNNs localize the same morphological discriminators (thorax, abdomen) used by expert entomologists. Lee et al. extended this by fusing RGB and UV-fluorescent images through a Swin-Transformer + Faster R-CNN pipeline on a Jetson AGX Xavier, though their physical trap was not designed for sex-based sorting or edge-only deployment. Wilkes et al. demonstrated that commodity CMOS sensors can be chemically modified to capture UV imagery for roughly USD 25, confirming that low-cost hardware is viable for mosquito imaging.

Smart-trap systems. Liu et al. deployed a rotating-cylinder sorting mechanism on a Raspberry Pi 3 with SqueezeNet, demonstrating the feasibility of lightweight classifiers with physical specimen routing, but without ONNX runtime optimization or end-to-end IoT monitoring. Oliveira et al. integrated YOLOv7 on a Raspberry Pi 4 with multi-duct wind selection and LoRaWAN cloud connectivity, enabling real-time monitoring and selective release, but without sex classification or physical sex-sorted retention. Paul et al. validated low-cost fluorescent markers for mark–release–recapture studies, confirming that field entomology workflows can rely on accessible materials, though their approach requires off-device microscopy and provides no AI-based inference.

Institutional context (FIUNA). The present work also builds on a local line of undergraduate theses at FIUNA oriented toward mosquito detection and classification. Honge Kudo (2025) developed the first functional mechatronic prototype, combining vacuum-turbine suction with a rotating cylinder for physical separation and acoustic sensing (microphone + STFT), though capture was still manually triggered. Zichner von Schmeling (2026) replaced audio sensing with an infrared wingbeat-frequency sensor (OPT101) immune to ambient noise, adding a custom PCB and BLDC-driven suction. Balbuena Duarte and Barrios Pereira (2026) introduced visual sex classification with YOLOv8, a servo-controlled iris capture mechanism, and a fermentation-based CO₂ lure, explicitly recommending migration to on-device edge AI and IoT connectivity as future work.

None of the reviewed works — international or institutional — simultaneously provides (i) sex-based physical sorting, (ii) a cascaded deep-learning pipeline exported to ONNX for low-latency edge inference, and (iii) an end-to-end IoT stack for cloud monitoring, all within a single low-cost embedded platform. Closing this gap is the core contribution of the present work.

III. Mechanical Design

Biological Lure

The attractant reproduces the CO₂ profile of human respiration — the primary host-location cue for Ae. aegypti — through yeast fermentation in a gelified sucrose medium. The gel is prepared from water, sugar, and neutral gelatin, activated with baker's yeast, and sealed into a 1.5 L container connected to the capture duct. Once activated, the reaction releases CO₂ continuously at 4.5–9 g/day, for a total of 40–70 g over an 8-day cycle before the batch is exhausted and must be reactivated. Because the fermentation rate is temperature-dependent, CO₂ output naturally peaks during warmer hours — which coincides with periods of highest mosquito host-seeking activity — and slows during cooler nights, helping the batch last through its full cycle.

Enclosure and Airflow

The trap enclosure is a 220×160×45 mm³ rectangular prism, 3D-printed in PLA for its ease of fabrication, mechanical durability, and low mass. A transparent acrylic front plate, secured with 3 mm screws, allows unobstructed optical inspection of the capture zone. Capture is driven by negative pressure: a central 20×20 mm square duct runs vertically through the unit, with a conical transition narrowing a 72 mm circular inlet down to the duct cross-section, and a 92 mm PC fan (40 CFM nominal) generating suction at the base. Under the estimated operating point (~15% of nominal flow due to duct restriction), inlet velocity is a gentle ∼0.69 m/s — enough to draw insects in without deterring their approach — while duct velocity accelerates to ∼7.05 m/s, above the mosquito's typical flight speed (1.0–1.5 m/s) and sufficient to prevent escape once inside.

Rotary Sorting Mechanism

At the mid-section of the duct, a 140 mm-diameter rotating cylinder is mounted perpendicular to the airflow axis. A hollow sliding inner tube, capped at its upstream end with a 250 µm nylon mosquito mesh, holds the specimen in the imaging position; its downstream end is left open to allow gravity ejection into the corresponding compartment after classification. A continuous-rotation servomotor (HK15138), mounted at the rear of the enclosure, drives the cylinder, while three embedded neodymium magnets and a Hall-effect sensor (A3144) provide closed-loop feedback at each of the three fixed angular stops. The full capture-to-reset sequence takes approximately 2.1 s:

  1. Capture — the fan draws the mosquito through the inlet and into the cylinder, where airflow pins it against the mesh.
  2. Identification — the specimen is illuminated and imaged against the white PLA background; the vision pipeline classifies it by sex.
  3. Segregation — the servo rotates the cylinder to align its inner duct with the corresponding compartment.
  4. Gravity ejection — the inner tube slides down under gravity, acting as a plunger that pushes the mosquito into the selected compartment.
  5. Reset — the cylinder and inner tube return to the capture position, ready for the next cycle.

Electronic Architecture & Hardware

The electronics follow a master–slave architecture. A Raspberry Pi 5 acts as the master: it captures the frame through the Camera Module 3 NoIR, runs the detection–classification pipeline, and sends the resulting sorting order (compartment A or B) to an Arduino UNO R3 over UART. The Arduino acts as the real-time slave, translating that order into a PWM command for the HK15138 servo and logic signals for the relay and status LEDs, while continuously reading back the BME280 environmental sensor and the A3144 Hall sensor used for rotor position feedback.

Power is organized into two isolated domains. The raw 12 V input is switched directly to the 92 mm fan through a relay, keeping that noisier, higher-current load off the control electronics; an LM2596 step-down converter derives a regulated 5 V rail from the same 12 V source to power the Arduino, servo, and relay logic. The Raspberry Pi 5 is powered independently through its own USB-C supply, which prevents brownouts or resets caused by the current draw of the fan and servo.

IV. Detection & Sex Classification Pipeline

Strategy

The system targets automated sex classification of Ae. aegypti on edge hardware — a task distinct from the species-level detection or discrimination addressed by most prior smart traps. It adopts a two-stage cascaded architecture: a single-class YOLOv8 detector first localises each specimen in the frame, following the real-time detection scheme validated on Raspberry Pi by Oliveira and Mafra; a MobileNetV3 Large classifier then discriminates the sex of each detected specimen, building on the capacity of CNNs to capture fine morphological features demonstrated by Park et al. Both models are exported to ONNX for efficient inference on the embedded device. Decoupling detection from classification allows each component to be optimised independently and gives the system natural scalability: a third species-discrimination stage could later be added at only marginal training and inference cost, since it would operate exclusively on detections already filtered by the first two stages. Training and metric evaluation were carried out on a workstation with an Intel Core i7 (14th gen) CPU and an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 5060 Laptop GPU; inference times on the embedded device itself are reported at the end of this section.

YOLOv8 Detection Model

Dataset. The detection dataset combines Roboflow Universe imagery with manually annotated field captures under a single mosquito class — a choice that simplifies the model and its later ONNX export. Three augmented variants were generated per image (90° rotations, brightness ±19%, pixel noise up to 1.45%), yielding 22,309 images in total (87.5% train / 6.3% validation / 6.2% test; 17,140 labelled instances of the mosquito class).

Hyperparameter Search. Hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum, weight decay, warm-up epochs, loss-term weights, and augmentation strength) were tuned with Optuna's TPE sampler over 100 trials of 15 epochs each, maximising mAP@50 on the validation set; the best trial (mAP@50 = 0.963) was reached at trial 43, confirming strong achievable performance ahead of the final training run.

PyTorch Results. Using the optimal configuration, the final YOLOv8s model was fine-tuned for 200 epochs (256×256 images, batch 16, GPU, early-stopping patience 20, mosaic augmentation disabled in the last 10 epochs), with deterministic seeding for reproducibility. Training and validation losses decreased steadily with no sign of overfitting, and the model reached mAP@50 = 0.975 (precision = recall = 0.954, mAP@50-95 = 0.734) on the held-out test set, with a low false-negative count (1,068 TP / 57 FP / 30 FN) — an important property for a monitoring application where missed detections are especially costly.

MobileNetV3 Classification Model

Dataset. The classification dataset originates from mosquito images labelled by sex in the prior FIUNA thesis of Balbuena Duarte and Barrios Pereira, provided by the IICS. Cropping each annotated bounding box into an individual, centred specimen image — via a script that validates crop bounds and discards invalid regions — converted the original 2,571-image detection-style dataset into 5,343 single-specimen images (4,403 train / 493 validation / 447 test). Extensive augmentation (flips, 90°/180° and random ±45° rotations, shear, saturation/brightness/exposure jitter, slight blur) was applied to the training split only. The training and validation splits are moderately imbalanced toward the female class (61.9% and 64.7% respectively), while the test split is near-balanced (50.8% female / 49.2% male) for an unbiased evaluation.

Training. MobileNetV3 Large was initialised from ImageNet weights (transfer learning), with its classification head replaced by a two-output linear layer. Images were resized to 160×160 (batch 32) and trained with AdamW (lr = 3×10-4, weight decay 10-4), a cosine-annealing learning-rate schedule, and cross-entropy loss with inverse-frequency class weights and label smoothing (α = 0.1) to counteract the class imbalance and reduce overconfidence. Training ran for up to 50 epochs with early stopping (patience 10), retaining the checkpoint with the best validation accuracy.

On the test set, the model reached 97.31% accuracy, with only 4 false negatives on females and 8 on males across all 447 samples — a balanced result with no systematic bias toward either class.

Cascade Model

Each camera frame is processed end-to-end through the following sequential stages:

  1. Letterbox resize — the frame is resized to 256×256 preserving aspect ratio via symmetric padding.
  2. Detection — the YOLOv8s ONNX graph predicts bounding boxes with confidence scores.
  3. Decode & NMS — predictions are filtered (confidence ≥ 0.125) and deduplicated via Non-Maximum Suppression (IoU = 0.45).
  4. ROI extraction — each surviving box is cropped from the original, un-resized frame.
  5. Crop preprocessing — every crop is resized to 160×160 and normalised with ImageNet statistics.
  6. Classification — the MobileNetV3 ONNX graph produces a Female/Male logit pair for each crop.
  7. Argmax — the higher-scoring class is assigned as the final sex label.

Fig. 11. Representative cascade output: YOLOv8s bounding boxes followed by MobileNetV3 sex labels for each detected specimen.

Evaluated end-to-end on the classifier's 447-image test set, the cascade reaches a conditional classification precision of 94.69% (321 correct out of 339 valid detections) — close to the classifier's isolated 97.31%, confirming that MobileNetV3 remains robust on automatically generated crops; the residual 2.6-point gap reflects geometric imperfections in YOLO-generated crops rather than any degradation of the classifier itself. Detection recall is markedly lower in this setting (75.84% vs. 93.9% on the detector's own native test set) — both models are evaluated on the same 447 single-specimen crops, a distribution the detector never saw during training, unlike the classifier, which is evaluated on its native domain. The resulting overall system precision of 71.81% therefore points to the detector's training set as the main avenue for improvement, rather than to any architectural limitation of the cascade.

The complete system was field-deployed autonomously for six consecutive days (Barrio Ycuá Satí, Asunción, Paraguay) on wild Ae. aegypti populations, capturing 21 events (16 female, 5 male) with 2 false positives — a field precision of 90.5% without any environment-specific retraining. A temperature drop from 30°C to 18°C on days 4–6 produced an observable reduction in capture rate, consistent with known Aedes activity patterns, and all detection events were streamed to the cloud dashboard alongside BME280 environmental data throughout the trial.

V. IoT Architecture

Downstream of the inference pipeline, the system follows a three-layer client–server architecture that persists, transmits, and visualises the entomological data generated by the trap. Each trap's Raspberry Pi is the edge client: it runs the local inference pipeline and pushes its results to a central backend server (FastAPI + Uvicorn), which a static frontend dashboard then queries for near-real-time display. A dynamic config.js file and CORS middleware decouple the three layers, so client devices, server, and dashboard can be scaled or replaced independently — including running several Raspberry Pi traps as concurrent clients of the same backend.

Edge Client (Raspberry Pi) & Backend

On each Raspberry Pi client, a finite-state machine (IDLE / SORTING) drives a multi-threaded pipeline that separates image capture, inference, and HTTP transmission so none blocks the others, discarding frames when the inference thread is busy rather than queuing them; the onboard Arduino, reached over a local UART link, only handles the physical sorting actuation and is not itself a network client. On the backend, every incoming record is tagged with a client_id identifying which Raspberry Pi trap produced it, so a single server can support several traps at once: metadata (client ID, timestamp, inference results, temperature, humidity) is stored in a PostgreSQL database managed by Neon, while each captured image is persisted in a Supabase Storage bucket and linked to its record via a public URL.

Communication Protocol & Dashboard

Each Raspberry Pi client combines three communication levels running in parallel: non-blocking queues between its internal threads; a local serial UART link to the onboard Arduino (9600 baud, one character per valid detection) that triggers mechanical actuation; and HTTP requests to the backend, sent from an independent thread with a cooldown to avoid server saturation — keeping actuation and data transmission mutually non-blocking.

Fig. 14. Raspberry Pi client communication protocol: internal, serial (UART), and HTTP levels operating in parallel.

The frontend polls /api/dashboard-data periodically, rendering detections, sex classification, and environmental readings from every connected Raspberry Pi client in near real time. It is deployed on Vercel (global CDN), with the backend on Railway (Dockerised FastAPI/Uvicorn) — both with continuous delivery from the Git repository.

Semi-Field Trials

Two 36-hour trials assessed the trap under controlled conditions with laboratory-reared specimens, before the system was exposed to a real, uncontrolled environment in the field trial that follows.

Enclosure and Conditions. Both trials ran in the same sealed, climate-controlled room — the Medical Equipment Laboratory at FIUNA, San Lorenzo (5.8 m × 2 m) — held at 21–25°C and 40–60% relative humidity throughout. The trap was placed in one corner of the room next to a sugar-water source, keeping released specimens active and within reach of the device. Each trial used 66 IICS-donated Ae. aegypti (33 female / 33 male, 3–7 days old): food was withheld for 4 hours before release to encourage host-seeking behaviour, specimens were then released and given 30 minutes to disperse through the room before the trap was activated, and it was left running for 36 hours with every detection logged automatically. Not every released specimen left the transport container on its own, so the effective population (Neff = Ntotal − Ninactive) — 60 of 66 in Trial 1, 55 of 66 in Trial 2 — is used as the denominator for the capture ratio below.

Results were consistent across both trials, with identical classification precision and retention. The moderate capture ratio (27–29%) reflects that a meaningful share of the effective population never engaged with the trap during the 36-hour window, pointing to attractant strength as the main lever for future improvement rather than the detection or sorting logic.

Failure Modes. Three failure patterns emerged consistently across both trials:

  1. Sex misclassification — one female was classified as male in each trial, attributable to partial occlusion or to transit speed through the suction channel exceeding the camera's frame rate.
  2. Mechanical escapes — every escape involved a female, traced to a small gap between the rotating cylinder and the acrylic plate that the smaller-bodied specimens could slip through.
  3. Limited capture — a meaningful share of Neff never engaged with the trap, pointing to attractant strength, rather than detection or sorting accuracy, as the main lever for improvement.

Field Trial

The prototype was then deployed autonomously for six consecutive days (1–7 May) at a residence in Barrio Ycuá Satí, Asunción, mounted against a wall under eave cover, 8 m from the street. It logged 21 mosquito detections (16 female, 5 male) plus one non-mosquito (a Geometridae moth, excluded from the precision count) and 2 verified false positives, giving a field precision of (21−2)/21 ≈ 90.5% — achieved with no hyperparameter tuning or retraining for the outdoor environment.
